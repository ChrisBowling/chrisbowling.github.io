Poverty on Pine Ridge

As a reservation, Pine Ridge has statistics that mirror those of developing countries. All of this leads to more poverty and earlier death.

By Chris Bowling

The Pine Ridge Reservation is a place of natural wonder and humbling beauty. Sectioned off within an immense sea of plains in the southwestern corner of South Dakota, the reservation is home to wide open prairie, interrupted only by ragged, pine-covered bluffs. As one drives north into this reservation, they’ll notice flat plains transition into spires and cliff faces of clay-rich rock formations—the Badlands.

But while its geography tells the story of an unsullied land, its people reveal something much different.

On this South Dakota reservation, the Oglala Lakota live in the poorest of America’s 3,144 counties, according to a 2014 U.S. Census Bureau report.

In addition, Pine Ridge isn’t just the most poverty-stricken area of the country—it sticks out from a stretch of low-poverty areas like sore thumb. If one were to look at this map without any knowledge of who lives in these counties, they might be hard-pressed to find an answer to how a random population of people in the middle of the nation could struggle while their neighbors seem to get along OK. But when one looks at how poverty stacks up in the rest of the state, it’s clear that there’s a pattern.

In this pattern of poverty afflicting native communities, Pine Ridge and its neighboring reservations aren’t alone. Counties with majority native populations almost all fall above the national poverty rate.

That, along with other conditions, adds to a poor life expectance. According to Rainey Enjady, former interim CEO of the Pine Ridge Hospital, men die on average at age 47. That’s a shorter lifespan than any other country in the world—two years shorter according to the World Health Organization. Its women fare better. On average, they live to 55, on par with Angola, Nigeria and Somalia.

Death also comes in many fashions on Pine Ridge. One that consistently takes lives, young and old, male and female, is suicide. Since December 2014, 1,053 people have planned, attempted or committed suicide. The rate of those who succeed is about 4.6 times the national rate.
